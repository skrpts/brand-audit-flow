## Voice Consistency Evaluation Criteria

A brand voice audit measures the gap between a brand's *stated* voice (what they say they sound like) and their *actual* voice (what they genuinely sound like across real communications). The goal is not to judge the voice itself, but to assess whether it is applied consistently and whether it differentiates the brand meaningfully.

### What to Collect Before Auditing
- The brand's own voice/tone guidelines (if they exist)
- 15–25 samples across at least 4 channels (website, social, email, advertising, support, packaging)
- 5–10 competitor samples for differentiation comparison
- Any internal style guides, brand books, or training materials

### What Consistency Means
A consistent voice does not mean every piece sounds identical. It means a reader could identify the brand from the writing alone — across channels, across content types, across authors. Appropriate tonal variation (more casual on social, more measured in legal comms) is expected and healthy. The *personality* should remain recognisable.

## Brand Voice Dimensions to Audit

Evaluate each dimension independently. A brand may score well on vocabulary consistency but poorly on structural consistency.

### 1. Tone
The emotional register of the writing. Is it warm, clinical, playful, authoritative, irreverent? Does the intended tone come through clearly, or does it shift unpredictably between pieces?

**What to look for:** Adjective and adverb choices, sentence mood (declarative vs interrogative vs exclamatory), use of humour, level of formality, emotional distance from the reader.

### 2. Vocabulary
The specific word choices and terminology patterns. Does the brand use distinctive language consistently? Are there signature phrases, preferred terms, or avoided words?

**What to look for:** Jargon levels, technical vs plain language balance, recurring phrases, brand-specific terminology, consistency of product/feature naming, use of colloquialisms.

### 3. Sentence Structure
The rhythm and construction of the writing. Short, punchy sentences create a different feel from long, flowing ones. This dimension is often overlooked but contributes significantly to voice recognition.

**What to look for:** Average sentence length, use of fragments, paragraph length, list frequency, question frequency, use of em-dashes/parentheticals, punctuation patterns.

### 4. Personality
The human characteristics that come through in the writing. If the brand were a person, could you describe them consistently across all samples?

**What to look for:** Level of self-reference, use of "we" vs "the company", willingness to express opinions, sense of humour style, how they handle negative topics (complaints, limitations, bad news), degree of formality in greetings/sign-offs.

## Scoring Rubric for Voice Consistency

Rate each dimension on a 1–5 scale across the full sample set.

### Score 1 — No Consistency
The voice changes dramatically between pieces with no discernible pattern. Different channels or authors appear to be writing for entirely different brands. No recognisable personality emerges. The stated voice guidelines (if they exist) bear little relationship to reality.

### Score 2 — Weak Consistency
Occasional flashes of a recognisable voice, but significant drift is common. One or two channels may be consistent internally but diverge sharply from others. The voice often defaults to generic corporate language. Guidelines exist but are not being followed reliably.

### Score 3 — Moderate Consistency
A recognisable voice exists and comes through in the majority of samples. Some channels or content types are noticeably off-voice. The brand could be identified by a familiar reader roughly half the time in a blind test. This is where most brands land.

### Score 4 — Strong Consistency
The voice is clearly identifiable across channels and content types. Tonal variation exists but the underlying personality remains constant. Minor inconsistencies appear in edge cases (legal content, crisis comms, third-party authored content). A familiar reader could identify the brand in a blind test most of the time.

### Score 5 — Exceptional Consistency
The voice is unmistakable. Every piece of communication reinforces the same personality, regardless of channel, author, or content type. Even functional content (error messages, confirmation emails, form labels) carries the voice. The brand would be identifiable in a blind test with high confidence. Very few brands achieve this.

## Common Voice Inconsistencies and Their Causes

| Inconsistency | Typical Cause | How It Manifests |
|---------------|---------------|------------------|
| Channel-to-channel voice shift | Different teams own different channels with no shared guidelines | Website sounds corporate; social sounds like a different brand entirely |
| Author-dependent voice | No voice training or editorial review process | Tone and vocabulary change noticeably depending on who wrote the piece |
| Formality mismatch | Guidelines don't address the full range of content types | Marketing copy is on-brand but support responses default to stiff, generic language |
| Inconsistent terminology | No glossary or product naming conventions | The same feature called different things across website, help docs, and ads |
| Personality loss under pressure | No guidance for sensitive or negative contexts | Brand is warm and witty in marketing but turns cold and legalistic in complaints or crisis comms |
| Voice drift over time | Original voice champion has left; no ongoing governance | Recent content sounds different from content produced 12–18 months ago |
| Agency vs in-house gap | External agencies interpret the brief differently from internal teams | Campaign creative sounds polished but disconnected from the brand's day-to-day voice |

## Competitive Voice Differentiation Analysis

### Framework

For each competitor, evaluate:

1. **Voice positioning:** Where do they sit on the key dimensions (formal–casual, technical–plain, serious–playful, distant–intimate)?
2. **Distinctiveness:** Could you identify this competitor's writing in a blind test? What makes it recognisable (or forgettable)?
3. **Category conventions:** What voice traits are common across the industry? Which brands conform, and which diverge?
4. **White space:** Are there voice positions no competitor occupies? Is there an opportunity for differentiation?

### How to Map It

Create a simple 2x2 matrix using the two voice dimensions most relevant to the category. For example:

- **X-axis:** Formal ← → Casual
- **Y-axis:** Technical ← → Accessible

Plot the audited brand and 3–5 key competitors. Clusters indicate undifferentiated voice positions. Open quadrants suggest potential differentiation opportunities — but only pursue them if the position is authentic to the brand.

### Differentiation Red Flags
- The brand occupies the exact same voice position as its closest competitor
- The brand's voice is indistinguishable from generic category language
- Competitors have recently shifted into the brand's previously distinctive territory
- The brand's voice position conflicts with its product positioning (e.g., premium product with bargain-bin copy)

## Channel-Specific Voice Audit Checklist

### Website
- [ ] Homepage headline and subheading reflect stated voice
- [ ] Product/service descriptions use consistent vocabulary
- [ ] About page personality matches the rest of the site
- [ ] CTAs use consistent language and tone
- [ ] Error pages, 404s, and system messages carry the voice
- [ ] Navigation labels and microcopy are on-brand
- [ ] Blog/content hub voice aligns with core site voice

### Social Media
- [ ] Post copy reflects brand personality (not just the social manager's personality)
- [ ] Response and comment voice is consistent with post voice
- [ ] Tone adapts appropriately across platforms without losing identity
- [ ] Hashtag and caption style is consistent
- [ ] Paid social copy matches organic voice

### Email
- [ ] Subject lines have a recognisable style
- [ ] Greeting and sign-off conventions are consistent
- [ ] Transactional emails (confirmations, receipts) carry the voice
- [ ] Newsletter tone matches marketing email tone
- [ ] Unsubscribe and preference pages maintain the voice

### Advertising
- [ ] Headline voice is consistent across campaigns
- [ ] Body copy voice matches website and other channels
- [ ] CTA language follows established patterns
- [ ] Different ad formats (search, social, display) show appropriate tonal variation while maintaining identity

### Customer Support
- [ ] Opening and closing phrases are on-brand
- [ ] Explanations use the brand's preferred vocabulary
- [ ] Difficult conversations (complaints, refunds) maintain the voice
- [ ] Automated responses and chatbot language reflect the brand
- [ ] Help documentation tone matches the broader brand voice

### Print and Packaging
- [ ] Product copy uses consistent vocabulary
- [ ] Instructional text carries the voice
- [ ] Legal and compliance text is as on-brand as regulations allow
- [ ] Physical touchpoints (packaging inserts, thank-you cards) reinforce the voice
