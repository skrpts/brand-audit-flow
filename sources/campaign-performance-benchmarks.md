## Brand Health Metrics

These metrics measure how a brand is perceived in the market. They are typically gathered through surveys, panel studies, or third-party research platforms.

| Metric | Poor | Below Average | Average | Good | Excellent |
|--------|------|--------------|---------|------|-----------|
| **Unaided brand awareness** | < 5% | 5–15% | 15–30% | 30–50% | 50%+ |
| **Aided brand awareness** | < 30% | 30–50% | 50–70% | 70–85% | 85%+ |
| **Brand consideration** | < 10% | 10–20% | 20–35% | 35–50% | 50%+ |
| **Brand preference** (vs alternatives) | < 5% | 5–15% | 15–25% | 25–40% | 40%+ |
| **Net Promoter Score (NPS)** | < 0 | 0–20 | 20–40 | 40–60 | 60+ |
| **Customer satisfaction (CSAT)** | < 60% | 60–70% | 70–80% | 80–90% | 90%+ |
| **Brand trust score** | < 40% | 40–55% | 55–70% | 70–85% | 85%+ |

**Context matters.** These ranges are cross-industry generalisations. A 20% unaided awareness is excellent for a niche B2B SaaS company but concerning for a national consumer brand. Always benchmark against category peers, not just absolute scales.

## Campaign Effectiveness Benchmarks by Objective

### Awareness Campaigns
Designed to increase brand recognition and recall. Typically measured over 4–12 week flights.

| Metric | Below Average | Average | Good | Excellent |
|--------|--------------|---------|------|-----------|
| Brand lift (awareness) | < 3 pts | 3–5 pts | 5–10 pts | 10+ pts |
| Ad recall lift | < 5 pts | 5–10 pts | 10–15 pts | 15+ pts |
| Reach efficiency (CPM) | > £15 | £8–£15 | £4–£8 | < £4 |
| Video view rate (15s+) | < 20% | 20–35% | 35–50% | 50%+ |
| Share of voice increase | < 2 pts | 2–5 pts | 5–10 pts | 10+ pts |

### Engagement Campaigns
Designed to deepen the audience's relationship with the brand. Measured by interaction quality, not just volume.

| Metric | Below Average | Average | Good | Excellent |
|--------|--------------|---------|------|-----------|
| Engagement rate (social) | < 1% | 1–2.5% | 2.5–5% | 5%+ |
| Average time on content | < 30s | 30–60s | 60–120s | 120s+ |
| Content share rate | < 0.5% | 0.5–1.5% | 1.5–3% | 3%+ |
| Email click-to-open rate | < 8% | 8–12% | 12–18% | 18%+ |
| Return visit rate | < 10% | 10–20% | 20–35% | 35%+ |

### Conversion Campaigns
Designed to drive specific actions — purchases, sign-ups, enquiries.

| Metric | Below Average | Average | Good | Excellent |
|--------|--------------|---------|------|-----------|
| Conversion rate (search) | < 2% | 2–4% | 4–7% | 7%+ |
| Conversion rate (social) | < 1% | 1–2.5% | 2.5–5% | 5%+ |
| Conversion rate (email) | < 1% | 1–2.5% | 2.5–4% | 4%+ |
| Cost per acquisition | Varies by industry — see industry ranges below |
| Return on ad spend (ROAS) | < 2:1 | 2:1–4:1 | 4:1–8:1 | 8:1+ |
| Landing page conversion rate | < 2% | 2–5% | 5–10% | 10%+ |

## Competitive Benchmarking Framework

### Share of Voice (SOV)
The brand's proportion of total advertising activity in its category. Measured by spend, impressions, or mentions depending on data availability.

- **SOV > Share of Market (SOM):** Brand is investing ahead of its current position — typically associated with growth.
- **SOV = SOM:** Brand is maintaining its position — stable but not gaining.
- **SOV < SOM:** Brand is under-investing relative to its market position — vulnerable to erosion over time.

**Rule of thumb (Binet & Field):** For every 10 percentage points of excess SOV (SOV minus SOM), expect roughly 0.5% market share growth per year. This is a long-term average, not a guarantee.

### Share of Search
The brand's proportion of organic search volume for category terms. A proxy for brand salience that is freely available via Google Trends or search console data.

| Position | Interpretation |
|----------|---------------|
| Dominant (40%+ of category searches) | Category leader; competitors are being searched in relation to you |
| Strong (20–40%) | Well-established; meaningful brand consideration |
| Moderate (10–20%) | Present but not top-of-mind; reliant on category searches |
| Weak (< 10%) | Low salience; likely competing primarily on price or distribution |

### Social Share of Voice
The brand's proportion of social mentions, conversations, and engagement within its category.

| Metric | How to Measure | What It Tells You |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Mention volume share | Brand mentions / total category mentions | Raw visibility |
| Engagement share | Brand engagement / total category engagement | Audience resonance |
| Sentiment share | Positive mentions / total positive category mentions | Quality of visibility |
| Creator/influencer share | Brand partnerships / total category partnerships | Investment in advocacy |

## Year-over-Year Trend Analysis Standards

### What Constitutes a Meaningful Trend
- **Minimum 3 data points** to identify a trend (quarterly data = minimum 9 months; monthly data = minimum 3 months)
- **Consistent direction** — two consecutive movements in the same direction suggest a trend; one movement is noise
- **Magnitude matters** — a 1-point NPS change is within error margins; a 5-point change warrants attention

### Trend Classification

| Movement | Classification | Recommended Response |
|----------|---------------|---------------------|
| 3+ consecutive periods of improvement | **Positive trend** | Document what's working; maintain course |
| 1–2 periods of improvement | **Positive signal** | Monitor; too early to attribute to any single initiative |
| Flat (within ±2% of prior period) | **Stable** | Check whether stability is the goal — if growth is expected, investigate |
| 1–2 periods of decline | **Warning signal** | Investigate potential causes; compare against competitive and seasonal patterns |
| 3+ consecutive periods of decline | **Negative trend** | Requires action; root-cause analysis and strategic response |

### Adjusting for External Factors
Before attributing a trend to brand or campaign performance, account for:
- **Seasonality** — compare year-over-year same-period, not sequential periods
- **Market shifts** — new competitor entry, category disruption, regulatory changes
- **Measurement changes** — platform algorithm updates, tracking methodology changes (e.g., iOS privacy changes affecting attribution)
- **Economic conditions** — recession, inflation, or sector-specific downturns affecting the entire category

## Statistical Significance Thresholds

### For Survey-Based Metrics (Brand Health, NPS, CSAT)

| Sample Size (per group) | Minimum Detectable Difference (95% confidence) |
|------------------------|-------------------------------------------------|
| 100 | ±10 percentage points |
| 250 | ±6 percentage points |
| 500 | ±4 percentage points |
| 1,000 | ±3 percentage points |
| 2,000+ | ±2 percentage points |

**Practical guidance:** Most brand tracking studies use sample sizes of 250–500 per wave. This means differences smaller than 4–6 percentage points between waves may not be statistically significant, even if they appear meaningful. Do not build strategy on changes within the margin of error.

### For Campaign Metrics (CTR, Conversion Rate, Engagement)
- **A/B tests:** Require a minimum of 1,000 conversions per variant to detect a 10% relative difference at 95% confidence. Smaller sample sizes can only detect larger differences.
- **Before/after comparisons:** Account for time-based confounders. A 4-week pre/post comparison is the minimum; 8 weeks is preferable.
- **Year-over-year comparisons:** Ensure the comparison periods are equivalent in length, seasonality, and spend level.

## Industry-Specific Benchmark Ranges

### Technology / SaaS
| Metric | Typical Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Website conversion rate | 2–5% (free trial), 0.5–2% (paid) |
| Email open rate | 20–28% |
| Search ads CPC | £2–£8 |
| Social ads CTR | 0.8–1.5% |
| NPS | 30–50 (good), 50+ (excellent) |
| Customer acquisition cost | £50–£500+ (depends on ACV) |
| Churn rate (annual) | 5–10% (good), 10–15% (average) |

### Retail / E-commerce
| Metric | Typical Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Website conversion rate | 1.5–3.5% |
| Cart abandonment rate | 65–75% |
| Email open rate | 15–20% |
| Search ads CPC | £0.50–£2.50 |
| Social ads CTR | 1.0–2.5% |
| ROAS (paid social) | 3:1–6:1 |
| NPS | 40–60 (good), 60+ (excellent) |

### Financial Services
| Metric | Typical Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Website conversion rate | 2–5% (lead gen) |
| Email open rate | 22–28% |
| Search ads CPC | £3–£15 |
| Social ads CTR | 0.5–1.0% |
| Cost per lead | £30–£150 |
| NPS | 20–40 (average for sector), 40+ (good) |
| Trust score | Critical — below 60% is a strategic risk |

### Healthcare / Pharma
| Metric | Typical Range |
|--------|--------------|
| Website conversion rate | 1.5–4% (appointment/enquiry) |
| Email open rate | 22–30% |
| Search ads CPC | £1.50–£6.00 |
| Social ads CTR | 0.6–1.2% |
| Patient/customer NPS | 30–50 (average), 50+ (good) |
| Content engagement rate | Higher than most sectors — health content is high-intent |
| Regulatory constraints | Heavily affects creative options; benchmark against regulated peers only |

### Cross-Industry CPA Ranges

| Sector | Average CPA | Good CPA |
|--------|-------------|----------|
| Technology / SaaS | £50–£200 | < £50 |
| Retail / E-commerce | £10–£40 | < £10 |
| Financial services | £50–£150 | < £50 |
| Healthcare | £30–£80 | < £30 |
| Education | £20–£60 | < £20 |
| Professional services | £40–£120 | < £40 |

**Important:** CPA benchmarks are meaningless without considering customer lifetime value (LTV). A £200 CPA is excellent if the average customer generates £5,000 in revenue. Evaluate CPA in ratio to LTV — a healthy LTV:CPA ratio is 3:1 or higher.
